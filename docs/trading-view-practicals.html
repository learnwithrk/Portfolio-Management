6.1 Program 1: SMA Trend Identification & Dynamic Support/Resistance

Objective

Identify trend direction and find dynamic support/resistance levels using the Simple Moving Average (SMA) — applied here to Indian stock market (NSE/BSE) charts on TradingView.

Steps

Step 1: Go to TradingView and Open the Chart Section

  1. Open your browser and navigate to tradingview.com.
  2. Sign in to your account (or create one if you haven’t already — it’s free).
  3. From the top navigation bar, click on “Products” or directly click “Chart” to launch the chart workspace.

Step 2: Select an NSE Stock and Set the Timeframe

  1. Click on the symbol search bar at the top-left of the chart (it may currently show a default ticker).
  2. Type the stock name — for example, RELIANCE.
  3. From the dropdown results, make sure to select the listing tagged NSE (National Stock Exchange of India), since TradingView also lists the same company on other exchanges. It should appear as NSE:RELIANCE.
    • Other popular NSE examples you can try: NSE:TCS, NSE:HDFCBANK, NSE:INFY, NSE:ICICIBANK, NSE:SBIN.
  4. Once selected, the candlestick chart for Reliance Industries will load, denominated in ₹ (INR).
  5. Now set the timeframe for long-term investing analysis. Click on the timeframe selector in the top toolbar (it usually shows “1D” by default).
  6. Select 1W (1 Week) from the dropdown, or type 1W directly into the timeframe box and press Enter.
  7. The chart will now display weekly candles — each candle represents one full week of trading on NSE, which smooths out daily noise and is ideal for long-term trend analysis.

Why weekly timeframe for Indian long-term investing? NSE has frequent intraday volatility, especially around results season, RBI policy announcements, and budget days. A weekly chart filters this noise and reveals the broader trend more reliably for long-term positioning.

Step 3: Open the Indicators Panel

  1. Look at the top toolbar of the chart and click on “Indicators” (it may show as “Indicators” with an fx or function-style icon, sometimes abbreviated visually).
  2. A panel titled “Indicators & Strategies” will pop up, with a search bar at the top.
  3. In the search box, type “Simple Moving Average”.
  4. You’ll see one or more matching results in the list (TradingView sometimes shows it twice — once as a standalone built-in indicator, and once under community scripts). Use the built-in “Moving Average Simple” — it’s the official one maintained by TradingView and is sufficient for this exercise.

Step 4: Add the SMA and Set the Length to 50

  1. Click on “Moving Average Simple” from the search results. It will be added to your chart instantly with a default length (usually 9).
  2. To customize it, click the gear/settings icon next to the SMA name in the legend at the top-left of the chart (it appears once the indicator is added), or double-click directly on the SMA line on the chart.
  3. In the settings window that opens, find the “Length” field under the Inputs tab.
  4. Clear the existing value and type 50.
  5. Click OK to apply.
  6. The chart will now redraw with a 50-period SMA line overlaid on the weekly candles. Since you’re on the weekly timeframe, this represents the 50-week SMA — roughly the average closing price over the last ~50 weeks (about 11–12 months), which is a popular long-term trend filter among Indian long-term investors and positional traders.

Step 5: Interpret the SMA on the Chart

Once the SMA 50 is plotted, observe where the current price sits relative to the line:

  • Price Above SMA 50 → Uptrend: The stock is trading above its 50-week average, suggesting sustained buying interest and a bullish long-term structure. Many Indian portfolio managers use this as a basic “trend filter” before considering fresh long-term buys.
  • Price Below SMA 50 → Downtrend: The stock is trading below its 50-week average, signaling weakness or a corrective/bearish phase. This is often used as a caution flag to avoid fresh long-term entries or to review existing holdings.
  • SMA as Dynamic Support or Resistance: Unlike a fixed horizontal support/resistance line, the SMA moves with price over time. In an uptrend, the SMA 50 frequently acts as a “floor” where price pulls back to and bounces from. In a downtrend, it often acts as a “ceiling” that price rallies up to and gets rejected from.

Trading Rules

Signal Condition Action
🟢 BUY Price approaches the 50 SMA from above and bounces off it (forms a reversal candle near the line) Consider entering or adding to a long-term position
🔴 SELL Price approaches the 50 SMA from below and gets rejected (fails to close above it) Consider booking profits or avoiding fresh entry

Indian market context note: Apply this technique to liquid, fundamentally sound NSE large-cap or Nifty 50 stocks for long-term investing decisions. For small-cap or low-liquidity stocks, SMA bounces can be less reliable due to erratic price action and lower trading volumes. Always combine this technical signal with basic fundamental checks (sector outlook, quarterly results, promoter holding trends) before making investment decisions — this manual covers a technical tool, not complete investment advice.

Practice Exercise

  1. Open NSE:TCS on the weekly timeframe and add a 50 SMA.
  2. Identify whether the stock is currently in an uptrend or downtrend based on price position relative to the SMA.
  3. Scroll back through the last 2–3 years of price history and mark at least two instances where price bounced off the 50 SMA, and one instance where it was rejected by the SMA.
  4. Repeat the exercise for NSE:HDFCBANK and compare the trend structure between the two stocks.

Key Takeaways

  • The 50 SMA on a weekly chart is a simple, widely-used trend filter for long-term investing decisions on NSE-listed stocks.
  • Price position relative to the SMA (above vs. below) gives a quick read on the prevailing trend.
  • The SMA also acts as a dynamic support/resistance zone, offering potential entry (bounce) and exit (rejection) cues.
  • Always combine this with broader market context (Nifty 50 trend, sector performance) before acting on signals from a single stock chart.

6.2 Program 2: EMA Crossover Breakout Strategy

Objective

To identify breakout and momentum opportunities using the Exponential Moving Average (EMA) crossover technique — applied to Indian stock market (NSE/BSE) charts on TradingView.

Why EMA instead of SMA here? The Exponential Moving Average (EMA) gives more weight to recent price action compared to the SMA, making it react faster to new momentum. This makes it well-suited for spotting breakouts early — useful for Indian long-term investors who want to catch the start of a fresh up-move in stocks like Nifty 50 constituents.

Steps

Step 1: Open the Indicators Panel

  1. With your NSE stock chart open, click on “Indicators” in the top toolbar of the chart (shown with an fx-style icon, labeled “Indicators”).
  2. The “Indicators & Strategies” search panel will open.

Step 2: Search for “Moving Average Exponential”

  1. In the search bar at the top of the panel, type “Moving Average Exponential”.
  2. From the results list, select the built-in “Moving Average Exponential” indicator (maintained directly by TradingView, distinct from any community/script versions).

Step 3: Add the First EMA — Set Length to 20, Color Green

  1. Once selected, the EMA will be added to your chart with a default length (usually 9).
  2. Click the gear/settings icon next to the EMA’s name in the chart legend (top-left of the chart) to open its settings.
  3. Under the Inputs tab, change the Length field to 20.
  4. Switch to the Style tab and change the line color to Green for easy visual identification.
  5. Click OK to apply. Your chart now shows a 20-period EMA in green — on the weekly timeframe, this is your 20-week EMA, representing short-to-medium-term momentum.

Step 4: Duplicate the EMA to Add a Second One

  1. In the chart legend (top-left), right-click on the EMA 20 entry — or click the three-dot menu (⋮) next to it.
  2. From the dropdown menu, select “Duplicate”.
  3. This creates an exact copy of the EMA 20 indicator, stacked on the same chart, which you’ll now modify into a second, longer-period EMA.

Step 5: Configure the Second EMA — Set Length to 50, Color Red

  1. Click the gear/settings icon next to the newly duplicated EMA in the legend.
  2. Under the Inputs tab, change the Length from 20 to 50.
  3. Switch to the Style tab and change the line color to Red.
  4. Click OK to apply.
  5. Your chart now displays two EMAs:
    • EMA 20 (Green) — faster, tracks short-term momentum
    • EMA 50 (Red) — slower, tracks the broader trend

Step 6: Observe the EMA Crossovers on the Chart

With both EMAs plotted, watch for the points where the green (20) line crosses the red (50) line:

  • These crossover points are the core signal of this strategy and will appear at multiple points across the stock’s price history on NSE charts — particularly visible during strong trending phases like post-results rallies or sectoral rotations.

Interpretation

Crossover Type Visual Cue Meaning
🟢 20 EMA crosses above 50 EMA Green line moves above red line Bullish Breakout — short-term momentum is turning positive relative to the broader trend
🔴 20 EMA crosses below 50 EMA Green line moves below red line Bearish Breakout — short-term momentum is turning negative relative to the broader trend

Indian market context note: This is sometimes informally compared to a faster version of the “Golden Cross / Death Cross” concept widely followed in Indian financial media (where 50-day/200-day crossovers are reported for the Nifty 50 and Sensex). The 20/50 EMA crossover on a weekly chart is a more sensitive, earlier-warning version suited for individual NSE stocks rather than just benchmark indices.

Trading Rule

  • 🟢 BUY: Enter when there is a bullish crossover (20 EMA crosses above 50 EMA) AND the current price is trading above both EMAs. This double confirmation — crossover plus price position — helps filter out weak or false breakout signals that can occur in choppy, sideways NSE stock action.
  • 🔴 SELL / AVOID: Treat a bearish crossover (20 EMA crosses below 50 EMA) with price below both EMAs as a signal to avoid fresh entries or to review existing long positions in that stock.

Practice Exercise

  1. Open NSE:INFY on the Weekly timeframe and apply both the 20 EMA (green) and 50 EMA (red) as described above.
  2. Scroll through the last 2–3 years of price history and identify at least two bullish crossovers and one bearish crossover.
  3. For each bullish crossover found, check whether price was trading above both EMAs at that point — this confirms whether it met the full BUY rule.
  4. Repeat with NSE:SBIN and compare how frequently crossovers occur in a banking stock versus an IT stock — note any difference in reliability or noise.

Key Takeaways

  • The EMA reacts faster to price than the SMA, making the 20/50 EMA crossover a useful tool for spotting early breakout momentum in NSE stocks.
  • A crossover alone is not sufficient — always confirm with price position relative to both EMAs before acting, to avoid false signals in sideways markets.
  • This strategy works best on liquid, fundamentally strong stocks and during clearly trending market phases; it can generate frequent false signals during range-bound or highly volatile periods, which are common around Indian budget announcements, RBI policy days, and global macro events.
  • Combine EMA crossover signals with the broader trend context from Program 1 (SMA 50) for a more complete long-term investing view.

6.3 Program 3: VWAP Intraday Trend Strategy

Objective

To trade intraday using the Volume-Weighted Average Price (VWAP) on Indian stock market (NSE) charts on TradingView.

Important context shift: Unlike Programs 1 and 2, which used weekly charts for long-term trend analysis, this strategy is intraday — meaning all trading activity happens and is squared off within the same trading session. On NSE, the cash market trading session runs from 9:15 AM to 3:30 PM IST. If you place an intraday order (commonly labeled MIS — Margin Intraday Square-off — with most Indian brokers like Zerodha, Upstox, or Groww), it will be automatically closed out by the broker shortly before market close if you haven’t exited manually, typically around 3:15–3:20 PM IST. VWAP resets fresh at the start of every new trading day and is not meant to be analyzed across multiple days like the SMA/EMA in earlier programs.

Steps

Step 1: Set the Timeframe to 5 Minutes

  1. With your NSE stock chart open, click on the timeframe selector in the top toolbar (it may currently show “1D”, “1W”, or whatever was last used).
  2. Select 5m (5 Minutes) from the dropdown, or type 5 directly into the timeframe box and press Enter.
  3. The chart will now display 5-minute candles — each candle represents 5 minutes of trading activity within the NSE session. This shorter timeframe is essential for intraday strategies, as it captures the granular price-volume action needed for VWAP analysis.

Step 2: Open the Indicators Panel

  1. Click on “Indicators” in the top toolbar (shown with the fx-style icon).
  2. The “Indicators & Strategies” search panel will open with a search bar at the top.

Step 3: Search and Add “VWAP”

  1. In the search box, type VWAP.
  2. From the results, select the built-in “VWAP” indicator (officially maintained by TradingView).
  3. Click on it once to add it directly to your chart — no length or period setting is required, since VWAP is a session-based (intraday-anchored) indicator by default and recalculates automatically from the start of each trading session.

Step 4: VWAP Added to the Chart

  1. The VWAP line will now appear overlaid on your 5-minute candlestick chart.
  2. Notice that the VWAP line starts fresh each trading day from the 9:15 AM opening candle and builds up through the session as more price and volume data accumulates — it does not carry over from the previous day.
  3. As the day progresses, observe how price weaves above and below this line, and how the line itself smooths out compared to the choppier candle action.

Interpretation

Observation Visual Cue Meaning
🟢 Price Above VWAP Candles trading above the VWAP line Indicates institutional buying — large players (FIIs, DIIs, mutual funds) are likely accumulating at or above this average price, a bullish intraday signal
🔴 Price Below VWAP Candles trading below the VWAP line Indicates selling pressure — large sell orders are weighing on price below the session average, a bearish intraday signal
⚫ VWAP as Intraday Support/Resistance Price repeatedly touching and reacting to the VWAP line The VWAP line itself often behaves like a dynamic support (in an uptrending session) or resistance (in a downtrending session) level through the day

Why VWAP matters in the Indian intraday context: Institutional desks, mutual funds, and FII/DII trading desks frequently use VWAP as a benchmark to evaluate whether their own large block orders on NSE are executing at favorable prices. Because of this, VWAP tends to act as a genuine “fair value” reference point during the day — retail intraday traders watch it for the same reason, making it a self-reinforcing level.

Trading Rule

  • 🟢 BUY: Enter when price pulls back to the VWAP line and reverses upward — i.e., the stock dips down to touch or approach VWAP during an otherwise uptrending session, forms a reversal candle (such as a bullish engulfing or hammer-like candle), and resumes its move higher. This suggests the pullback found support at the institutional average price level.
  • 🔴 AVOID / EXIT: If price breaks decisively below VWAP and stays below it, treat this as a loss of intraday bullish momentum — consider exiting existing long positions rather than holding through the session.

A Note on Risk Management for Indian Intraday Trading

  • Since this is an intraday (MIS) strategy, always set a stop-loss below recent swing lows or a fixed percentage of capital risk per trade — NSE intraday volatility (especially around results, RBI announcements, or global cues like US Fed decisions) can move prices sharply within minutes.
  • Be mindful of margin and leverage rules set by SEBI and your broker for intraday (MIS) trades — leverage amounts vary by stock and broker, and positions must be squared off within the same session or they may be auto-closed by your broker’s risk management system (RMS), sometimes at unfavorable prices.
  • Avoid taking fresh intraday VWAP trades in the first 5–10 minutes after market open (9:15–9:25 AM), as this period often sees erratic price discovery before VWAP becomes a meaningful reference.

Practice Exercise

  1. Open NSE:HDFCBANK on the 5-minute timeframe during a live trading session (9:15 AM – 3:30 PM IST) and add the VWAP indicator.
  2. Track the stock for at least one hour and note every instance where price touches the VWAP line.
  3. Identify whether each touch resulted in a bounce (continuation of trend) or a breakdown (trend reversal).
  4. Repeat this observation on a different day with NSE:TCS and compare how VWAP behaves on a relatively calmer IT stock versus a more news-sensitive banking stock.

Key Takeaways

  • VWAP is a session-based intraday indicator that resets daily and reflects the true volume-weighted average price paid by all market participants during that NSE trading session.
  • Price position relative to VWAP gives a quick read on whether institutional money is net buying or net selling during the day.
  • VWAP pullback-and-reversal setups offer disciplined intraday entry points, but always pair them with a stop-loss given the leveraged nature of intraday trading.
  • This strategy is best suited to liquid, high-volume NSE large-cap stocks; thinly traded stocks can produce unreliable VWAP signals due to inconsistent volume.

6.4 Program 3B: Anchored VWAP for Long-Term Investing

Objective

To adapt the Volume-Weighted Average Price concept for long-term investing using Anchored VWAP (AVWAP) — a version of VWAP that does not reset daily, making it usable across weeks, months, and years on NSE stock charts.

Program 3 covered the standard session VWAP, which resets every trading day and is built specifically for intraday use — it has no meaningful interpretation on weekly or monthly charts. However, the underlying idea behind VWAP (the average price weighted by how much volume traded at each price level) is genuinely useful for long-term investors too — it just needs a different version of the tool.

Anchored VWAP (AVWAP) solves this. Instead of recalculating from the start of each day, you manually “anchor” it to a specific date you choose — such as a 52-week low, a major results announcement, or the start of a financial year — and it then calculates the volume-weighted average price continuously from that anchor point forward, for as long as you want to track it (weeks, months, or years).

Key distinction: Session VWAP answers “did today’s buyers get a fair price today?” Anchored VWAP answers “what is the average price paid by everyone who has bought this stock since this important date?” The second question is far more relevant to long-term investing.

Steps

Step 1: Set the Timeframe

  1. With your NSE stock chart open, click on the timeframe selector in the top toolbar.
  2. Select 1D (Daily) for a medium-term view, or 1W (Weekly) for a more zoomed-out long-term view — both work well with Anchored VWAP since, unlike session VWAP, it doesn’t depend on the candle interval.
  3. For this exercise, use the Daily (1D) timeframe, as it gives you finer control over picking an exact anchor date in the next step.

Step 2: Open the Indicators Panel

  1. Click on “Indicators” in the top toolbar (the fx-style icon).
  2. The “Indicators & Strategies” search panel will open.

Step 3: Search and Add “Anchored VWAP”

  1. In the search box, type Anchored VWAP.
  2. From the results, select the built-in “Anchored VWAP” indicator (maintained directly by TradingView — distinct from any community scripts with similar names).
  3. Click on it once. TradingView will typically place the anchor automatically at the leftmost visible candle on your current chart view as a starting default.

Step 4: Choose and Set Your Anchor Point

This is the most important step — the anchor date you pick determines what the AVWAP line represents. Common long-term investing anchor points for Indian stocks include:

  • 52-week low — to see the average price paid by everyone who has bought since the stock’s worst point in the last year (useful for spotting value re-accumulation).
  • Start of the financial year (April 1st) or calendar year (January 1st) — to track average cost basis for the current investing year, aligned with how Indian mutual funds and DIIs often report performance.
  • Day after a major quarterly results announcement — to see how the market has averaged price in reaction to that specific result.
  • A major budget day or RBI policy announcement — to study sustained reactions to macro events affecting Indian markets.

To set this:

  1. Click and drag the anchor marker directly on the chart to your chosen date, or
  2. Click the gear/settings icon next to “Anchored VWAP” in the chart legend, go to the Inputs tab, and manually enter the specific anchor date.
  3. Click OK to apply. The AVWAP line will now begin plotting from your selected anchor date and continue forward to the present day.

Step 5: Anchored VWAP Added to the Chart

  1. You will now see a single VWAP line starting from your chosen anchor point and extending across all subsequent daily (or weekly) candles, smoothing as more time passes.
  2. Unlike session VWAP, this line does not reset — it keeps calculating cumulatively from the anchor date until you change or remove it.
  3. You can add multiple Anchored VWAPs with different anchor dates on the same chart (e.g., one anchored to the 52-week low and another anchored to April 1st) by repeating Steps 3–4 and assigning each a different color for clarity.

Interpretation

Observation Visual Cue Meaning
🟢 Price Above Anchored VWAP Candles trading above the AVWAP line The average buyer since the anchor date is in profit — reflects sustained accumulation and bullish sentiment since that event
🔴 Price Below Anchored VWAP Candles trading below the AVWAP line The average buyer since the anchor date is at a loss — reflects distribution or weak sentiment since that event
⚫ AVWAP as Long-Term Support/Resistance Price repeatedly returning to and reacting off the AVWAP line The anchored level often acts as a “memory” price — a zone where long-term holders defend their average cost, creating real support or resistance

Indian market context: Anchored VWAP is particularly useful around results season (Q1–Q4 earnings on NSE) and around Union Budget day, since Indian institutional investors (FIIs/DIIs) often re-anchor their own cost-basis tracking to these dates. Watching where price sits relative to a results-day or budget-day AVWAP gives a sense of whether the broader market has digested that event positively or negatively over the following weeks and months.

Trading Rule

  • 🟢 BUY: Consider entering or adding to a long-term position when price pulls back to a meaningful Anchored VWAP (e.g., one anchored to the 52-week low or to the start of the financial year) and shows signs of holding/reversing upward — similar logic to the SMA 50 bounce rule in Program 1, but using a volume-weighted reference instead of a simple price average.
  • 🔴 CAUTION: If price decisively breaks below a long-standing Anchored VWAP that has acted as support for multiple weeks or months, treat this as a signal that the long-term holder base may be turning net negative — review the position or fundamental thesis before adding further.

Combining With Earlier Programs

This chapter works well alongside the tools from Programs 1 and 2:

  • Use SMA 50 (Program 1) for a general trend filter.
  • Use EMA 20/50 crossover (Program 2) for momentum confirmation.
  • Use Anchored VWAP (this chapter) anchored to a meaningful event (52-week low, financial year start, major results date) as a volume-aware reference for “fair value since that event” — adding a layer that pure moving averages don’t capture, since AVWAP factors in how much was traded at each price, not just the price itself.

Practice Exercise

  1. Open NSE:TCS on the Daily timeframe and add an Anchored VWAP anchored to its most recent 52-week low.
  2. Note whether the current price is above or below this AVWAP line, and what that implies about the average long-term buyer’s position.
  3. Add a second Anchored VWAP, this time anchored to April 1st of the current financial year, in a different color.
  4. Compare the two AVWAP lines — are they close together or far apart? What does the gap between them suggest about how sentiment has shifted across the year?
  5. Repeat with NSE:ICICIBANK, anchoring to its last quarterly results date, and observe how price has behaved relative to that post-results AVWAP since.

Key Takeaways

  • Standard session VWAP (Program 3) is strictly an intraday tool and resets daily — it cannot be meaningfully used for long-term analysis.
  • Anchored VWAP (AVWAP) solves this by letting you fix the starting point manually, making the volume-weighted average price concept usable across weeks, months, or years.
  • Choosing the right anchor date (52-week low, financial year start, major results/budget day) is what makes AVWAP meaningful — an arbitrary anchor produces an arbitrary line.
  • AVWAP works best as a complementary tool alongside SMA/EMA trend analysis, since it adds a volume dimension that simple moving averages do not account for.
